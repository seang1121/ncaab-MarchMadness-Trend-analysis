### Statistical Profile of Sweet 16 Teams

**KenPom Adjusted Efficiency:**
- Teams ranked in the **top 25 KenPom overall** reach the Sweet 16 at ~70-80% rate
- Teams outside the **top 50 KenPom** almost never reach Sweet 16 regardless of seed
- Every national champion in the KenPom era ranked in the **top 10 overall**

**The "Dual Elite" Profile â€” strongest combined signal:**
> Top 25 adjusted offensive efficiency AND top 25 adjusted defensive efficiency
> ~80%+ Sweet 16 rate. 5 of the past 10 champions fit this profile.

**Offense vs. Defense:**
- Both ends matter, but **offense has become more important** in the modern era
- All 5 champions (Virginia 2019, Baylor 2021, Kansas 2022, UConn 2023, UConn 2024) ranked **top 15 offensively** at tournament time
- Defense is the **floor** â€” teams with sub-top-50 defenses rarely survive two rounds regardless of offense
- Threshold for defense: **Top 30-40 = reliable Sweet 16 contender**
- Threshold for offense: **Top 40 = Sweet 16 viable, top 50 = minimum**

**Tempo/Pace:**
- No single tempo profile dominates for power programs â€” fast and slow teams both succeed
- For mid-major Cinderellas: **10 of 11 teams ranked outside the top 100 in adjusted tempo**
  - Slow tempo equalizes talent gaps in single-elimination games
  - Optimal balanced range: 65-72 possessions/game
  - Fast-tempo mid-majors (75+) have significantly worse Cinderella profiles

**Turnover Rate:**
- Sweet 16 teams consistently commit turnovers on **fewer than 17% of possessions**
- Forced turnover rate (steal rate) is an equalizer for mid-majors â€” teams that create 15%+ opponent TO rate have outsized upset potential
- Ball security is **critical** â€” March opponents scout intensely and pressure the ball

**3-Point Shooting â€” what the data says:**
- 3PT% is **too volatile** to rely on as a Sweet 16 predictor
- High-volume 3PT teams (40%+ of shots from deep) are boom-or-bust
- **3PT defense** (limiting opponent attempts, not just %) is more predictive
- Most consistent Sweet 16 teams: mid-range 3PT volume, high 2PT efficiency

## Part A â€” Why Teams Reach the Sweet 16: Statistical Factors

### A1 â€” The Efficiency Cliff: R32 â†’ Sweet 16

The R32 â†’ Sweet 16 jump is the single steepest efficiency cliff in the bracket. Teams that lost in R32 averaged AdjEM +14 to +16; teams that advanced to Sweet 16 averaged +22 to +24. That ~8-point AdjEM gap is the structural separation between "good enough to win one tournament game" and "built to go deep."

| Threshold | Floor | Why It's Required |
|-----------|-------|-------------------|
| AdjEM | +22 to +24 | Sub-+20 teams advance only with significant bracket luck |
| AdjD Rank | Top 40 | Every Sweet 16 team in 6 years had AdjD inside top 40 (Oral Roberts at AdjD #285 won R64 on offense, then exited R32 the moment they faced a real defense) |
| AdjO Rank | Top 35 | Sweet 16 requires scoring efficiency â€” defensive identity alone can carry R64 but rarely two consecutive games against prepared opponents |
| KenPom Overall | Top 25 | ~80% Sweet 16 rate for teams in this range; teams outside top 50 almost never advance regardless of seed |

### A2 â€” Why the 6-9 Seed Dead Zone Exists

Seeds 6-9 produced just 7.5% of Sweet 16 spots across 5 years â€” a structural bracket problem, not a talent problem.

- **6-seeds in R32**: Draw an 11-seed who won R1. That 11-seed is almost always a power conference at-large team (KenPom 25-50) â€” not a true underdog. A KenPom-30 team assigned an 11-seed is effectively a 4-6 seed in the bracket.
- **7-seeds in R32**: Draw a 10-seed who won R1, typically facing a 2-seed somewhere nearby. Difficult bracket placement both directions.
- **8-9 seeds in R32**: Win a coin-flip R1 game, then face a 1-seed â€” who advances at ~85-90%. The path is structurally near-impossible.
- **Result**: Not that 6-9 seeds are bad teams â€” it's that their bracket position forces them to beat more efficient opponents back-to-back with no margin for error.

### A3 â€” Why 11-Seeds Appear Every Year

An 11-seed reached the Sweet 16 every single year studied (2019â€“2024). The explanation is committee seeding error, not randomness:

Most 11-seeds that advance are power conference at-large teams with KenPom rankings of 25â€“40. Against a 6-seed in the same range, they are structural near-equals â€” but the line treats them as 5-point underdogs because of seed optics. When they win R1, they face a 3-seed in R32 (another power conference team they've played all season). The familiarity and quality removes the intimidation factor.

| Year | 11-seed that advanced | KenPom rank | Why they weren't a real Cinderella |
|------|-----------------------|-------------|-------------------------------------|
| 2019 | Ohio State | ~35 | Big Ten at-large â€” conference wins mattered |
| 2021 | UCLA | ~17 | KenPom top-20 â€” clearly mis-seeded |
| 2021 | Syracuse | ~45 | ACC at-large â€” experienced March program |
| 2022 | Iowa State | ~28 | Big 12 at-large â€” one of the best conferences |

### A4 â€” What True Cinderellas (Seeds 12-15) Who Reached Sweet 16 Had in Common

Three 15-seeds reached the Sweet 16 in this window. Each followed a statistically consistent profile:

| Team | Year | AdjD | Tempo | How They Won |
|------|------|------|-------|--------------|
| Oral Roberts | 2021 | ~285 | Slow | AdjO #25 exploited Ohio State's AdjD #79 (weak for 2-seed). Won by offensive mismatch, not defensive identity. Lost in Sweet 16 the moment they faced a team with a real defense |
| Saint Peter's | 2022 | ~28 | Very slow | AdjD top-30 + Kentucky's defensive weakness (weak AdjD for a 2-seed). Held 3 straight opponents within 4 points through pure defensive identity and pace control |
| Princeton | 2023 | ~13 | Very slow | AdjD #13 nationally â€” best defensive rank ever for a 15-seed. Held Arizona to 55 points on 4-for-25 from 3. Made every opponent play at Princeton's pace, not their own |

**The pattern**: Every non-power Cinderella that reached the Sweet 16 won by creating a specific statistical mismatch â€” either elite defense suffocating a offense-heavy opponent, or elite offense exploiting a defensively weak favorite. None won on overall talent.

### A5 â€” The Most Common Reason Chalk Teams Exit in R32

The single most consistent cause of a seeded favorite (2-through-5) losing in R32: **defensive ranking below expectations for their seed tier**.

When a 2-seed's AdjD ranks outside the top 40, or a 3-seed ranks outside the top 50, they become exploitable by teams that score efficiently. They can't stop teams they're supposed to beat. Ohio State 2021 (2-seed, AdjD #79), Kentucky 2022 (2-seed, weak AdjD), and Auburn 2024 (4-seed, AdjD vulnerable) all lost for this reason. The Sweet 16 defensive floor isn't aspirational â€” it's structural. Teams without AdjD top 40 almost never advance.

## Part 7 â€” Recommended Features for a Betting Model

### Tier 1 â€” Core Features (Highest Impact)
1. **KenPom Overall Rank** â€” numerical input; top 25 threshold flag
2. **Adjusted Defensive Efficiency Rank** â€” binary: top 30 = 1

9. **Conference tournament result** â€” champion or finalist = momentum signal
10. **Dual Elite boolean** â€” top 25 offense AND top 25 defense = championship contender

### Tier 3 â€” ATS-Specific Triggers
11. **Spread size** â€” 5+ in Sweet 16 = fade alert; 4-6 = underdog signal; 1-3 in R64 = avoid
12. **Round context** â€” R64 lean underdogs â†’ R32 lean chalk â†’ S16 lean underdogs
13. **11-seed flag vs 6-seed** â€” auto lean underdog
14. **Cinderella R2 fade flag** â€” if opponent won R1 as 12-15 seed, fade them in R2
15. **3PT volume flag** â€” 40%+ of shots from 3 = high-variance, avoid on totals
